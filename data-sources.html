Our Quality Assurance Process

  1. Source Verification: All data sources are verified for credibility, peer review status, and institutional backing
  2. Data Validation: Cross-referencing multiple sources to ensure consistency and accuracy
  3. Temporal Alignment: Standardizing data collection periods and adjusting for reporting delays
  4. Missing Data Handling: Systematic approaches to handle missing or incomplete data points
  5. Statistical Validation: Outlier detection and statistical consistency checks
  6. Expert Review: Regular validation by global health experts and epidemiologists

Research Methodology

Data Integration Process

  1. Source Identification: Systematic review of authoritative global health data sources
  2. Data Extraction: Automated and manual extraction processes with validation checks
  3. Standardization: Converting all data to common formats, units, and time periods
  4. Quality Assessment: Each dataset scored on completeness, accuracy, and timeliness
  5. Cross-Validation: Multiple source comparison for consistency verification
  6. Gap Analysis: Identifying and addressing missing data through interpolation or estimation
  7. Statistical Processing: Application of appropriate statistical methods for analysis
  8. Expert Review: Validation by subject matter experts and peer review process

Statistical Methods

Descriptive Analytics

Central tendency measures, dispersion analysis, and distribution assessment

  • Measures: Mean, Median, Mode
  • Dispersion: Standard Deviation, IQR
  • Distribution: Normality Testing

Inferential Statistics

Hypothesis testing, correlation analysis, and predictive modeling

  • Tests: t-tests, ANOVA, Chi-square
  • Correlation: Pearson, Spearman
  • Modeling: Regression Analysis

Limitations & Considerations

Data Limitations

  • Reporting Delays: Some countries may have delays in data reporting, affecting timeliness
  • Data Quality Variation: Different countries have varying levels of data collection infrastructure
  • Missing Data: Some indicators may not be available for all countries or time periods
  • Definition Differences: International standards may be interpreted differently across regions
  • Political Sensitivity: Some health data may be influenced by political considerations
